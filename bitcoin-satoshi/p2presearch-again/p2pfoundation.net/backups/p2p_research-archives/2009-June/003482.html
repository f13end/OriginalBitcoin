Hi Ryan,

I appreciate all your comments, and I sometimes share the feeling about
nihilism and a kind of hyper-criticality that disempowers. I agree, that
what is most empowering is what  you say, the generalisation of powerful
sharing practices which change human habits and thereby in the long run,
institutions, and this is indeed what p2p is all about.

But one cannot exclude thinking critically about it either ... my own
efforts have always been, not just on the ethos, but on seeing it as a way
to more radically change some of the structures of our world, which in my
opinion, are destroying it ...

Yes, there is more negativity, in a some way, your own reactions are part of
that, as are my own much less tolerant responses, I have noticed. Could it
be that the stress of the global crisis is affecting us, and that the
accumulation of difficulties both in personal life and society, creates a
much more easily triggered irritation level? That is my own guess, as I
don't feel anything else of style and substance has really changed that much
...

It also seems a human habit that positive news, like the links you forward,
are usually triggering less response, than negative developments ... the
media have known this ages ago ...

The solution might be to send out more empowering and positive initiatives
...

I appreciate enormously your continuous engagement with the list,

Michel

>>>> Simply put, classifying a good as rival or nonrival depends upon whether

>>>

>>>> consumption or use of the good by another.  You are confusing
>>>> excludability with rivalry.
>>>>
>>>> Information as a "good" is not rival, even if someone claims it private
>>>> (they can claim whatever they like).  Information can be used with no
>>>> rivalry.  Stallman was speaking of software.  Information--a subset being
>>>> software--is very clearly a non-rival good by definition.
>>>>
>>>> As for my own perspective: the divide between unenforceable and
>>>> untenable claims of rivalry, and the reality of information nonrivalry,
>>>> underlies the losing battle of copyrighted media "piracy." Legally
>>>> establishing nonrival goods as rival goods only creates more dissonance.
>>>> The term "intellectual property" attempts to beg the question by classifying
>>>> information as both private property and rival, but information is no more
>>>> rival property than "creation science" is science.  "Private" information
>>>> not released in some way is still a nonrival good, but is being excluded.
>>>>
>>>> Your points about Stallman were scattered, and in my view, incorrect.
>>>> The implicit lie about rivalry at the core of most "intellectual property"
>>>> claims couldn't be more relevant to P2P.
>>>>
>>>> Property laws consider the rivalrous/nonrivalrous nature of a good when
>>>> determining the entitlements they establish.  Any reading on the
>>>> jurisprudence behind property rights, whether Romer or Epstein, consider
>>>> both rivalry and excludability, among other important factors.
