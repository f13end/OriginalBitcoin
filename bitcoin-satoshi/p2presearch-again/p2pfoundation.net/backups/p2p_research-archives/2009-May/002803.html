Marc:

I recommend not destroying things others value just so as to have
opportunity to "fix" them.  Poor history of that.

Social systems crafting is profoundly difficult stuff.  Just ask those who
attempt to write constitutions, design economies, build civil society
organizations, etc. on a day to day basis.  Those who theorize such things
always find a neater world than the very messy one that actually exists.

For better or worse, there is an existing world out there.  We cannot ignore
it or destroy it.

What personally excites me about P2P is its capacity to retrofit sharing and
responsible approaches freely chosen regardless of the underlying politics
or economic models.  People give their time, energy and hope so that others
can move forward.  Sure, there is some Schadenfreude at seeing bad systems
hurt by good P2P systems, but that is a war that cannot, in my opinion, be
one.  If it becomes we/they, they always win.

Creative destruction is Schumpeter's awesome description of markets in
action.  He was right at a number of levels.  I disagree with his conclusion
that some form of socialism replaces all forms of capitalism, but that
remains to be seen.  It would be fine with me so long as it is reasonably
and freely chosen and continually and freely reaffirmed.

I also believe in path dependence.  Once headed in one direction, radical
shifts are both expensive and typically painful.

If I personally had to engage with any grand theory of the world economy,
I'd lean toward Karl Polanyi's Great Transformation though its conclusions
are a bit too socialist for me.

I personally think Kevin's thread toward a mutualist libertarianism is an
ideal portrait of P2P politics.  I've struggled to strike down libertarian
views my whole adult life...because I saw them as morally untenable.  The
more I work for government, the more libertarian I become.  What I am
certain of is that balance is necessary and difficult.  It requires
collaborative tools and agile leaders...much more common in civil society
than in governments.  So a mutualist, libertarian framework with free choice
and high moral normative values of trust and responsibility could work.
Getting there?  I'll leave that to the theorists.  We futurists deal with
what is going to happen, not what should happen.
