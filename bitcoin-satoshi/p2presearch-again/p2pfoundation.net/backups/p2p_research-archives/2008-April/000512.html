I long for the day when nation states are obsolete.

A time when where you come from what faith you have are irrelevant.

As a poetess rapper friend of mine Angel says:

"mother earth is the place of all our births"

To me global population will very obviously fall as availability of energy
falls (until we've all got our own open source solar concentrator CHP unit
of course!).

The only reason there are so many people on Earth now is because we've been
able to use massive amounts of fossil energy to help grow massive amounts of
food. More food, more people. Or more energy, more food, more people.

Of course, its not just our fossil energy supplies that are depleted either.
Fresh water (used mainly for agriculture) is increasingly scare. Soil
erosion is a massive global problem. With rising temperatures and falling
water supplies, grain harvests are falling. In recent month both India and
China stopped exporting grain.

As for energy, I just read this excellent article by Rob Hopkin's:

The Art of Falling Apart: do we ever learn from our mistakes? (Grangemouth,
peak oil and catastrophic hard drive failures)…
http://tinyurl.com/5spllc

I highly recommend his book, The Transition Handbook
http://uniteddiversity.com/transition-hankbook/

Smiles,

Josef.

> What is the conclusion? That populations will continue to shrink to the
> point of some kind of systemic failure in human systems?
>
> Populations could be likely to shrink, but they are then also likely to
> stabilize. Especially given the fact that we know the continuous growth of
> populations of humans is not infinitely sustainable, even though growth has
> been an overall trend in the last 1000+ years of human existence.
>
> Local conditions around the world will have a huge effect on the overall
> arc of growth or shrinkage. If there really is growth in China and India, as
> claimed by the original article in this thread, then this could have a
> stabilizing effect on the world-wide numbers.
>
> Also, if people in local cultures start focusing their economic activities
> *away* from unlimited and continuous growth, and ideology move away from
> religious "duty" to reproduce, and towards sustainable commons, then I think
> it is possible that it would be normal to see population growth rates
> stabilize. I think this is what is happening, really. I think it will end up
> being a natural stabilization on local levels over time (as opposed to a
> catastrophic drop that makes human systems unsustainable).
