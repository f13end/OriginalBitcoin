> degree at UT El Paso and vice versa.  So, when they realized they had this
> capability (and I have visited and met with them and they document it fairly
> rigorously...see http://www.wales.ac.uk/defaultpage.asp?page=e7062)
>
> As a trainor, I am interested in getting as much validation for my work as
> possible.  So I use facilities like the Institute of Leadership and
> Management validations, trade association validations, and am looking at
> using University of Wales validations.  I teach (or people train themselves
> in communities of practice), and students get a bucket of credentials.
>
> If I were a university, I'd hate me because I disaggregate their offerings
> and cherry-pick p2p relations with individual faculty, departments, classes
> etc.  This was how p2p started to erode business (e.g. Sun Microsystems).
> Institutions require command and control of aggregated product sets.  P2P
> melts that aggregation by allowing customization under the control of the
> peer.
>
> I saw IBM start to melt when they moved into open systems.  They have
> actually handled the transformation far better than most.  But I remember
> product meetings inside IBM where people said we were destroying the firm by
> moving away from Blue-only frameworks.  The same will be heard in
> universities.  But the moral imperative placed on faculty's shoulders is
> that they must recognize they are part of a corporate scheme, or they most
> open up.
>
> The argument of inability to judge quality at other institutions only goes
> so far in the instrumental education environment you portray.  Then they
> will claim their programs have special linked components.  But does anyone
> really believe that getting an MBA class in accounting at Dartmouth is
> systematically different than getting one at Penn State?  No.  The intent is
> to create elite associations.  Faculty, moral faculty, will only enable that
> so far once e-learning pervades and the place a student lives is less
> important.  For now, all sorts of institutional mechanics like alumni
> relations block that ethical transformation.  Even now very few faculty
> associate with their institution--they associate with their field.  The
> seeds of collapse are there.
