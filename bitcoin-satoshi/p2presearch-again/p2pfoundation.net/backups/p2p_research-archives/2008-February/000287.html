Actually, from what I can tell, the economy that is enabling the last mile
usable software in the realm of open source is at this time at least in part
an economy that is funded through things like:


   - commercial development of open source, which is releasing high
   quality software components that improve usability, but were produced to
   "scratch an itch" of a company or enterprise. Often seen in Drupal, PHP,
   Python and Ruby communities.
   - Bounties, or reverse bounties that are commissioned by developers or
   people who need the type of functionality you speak of, and realize that
   they can come in at a much lower cost factor by starting with an open source
   basis

The OSS communities often want to keep money motivations out of the
development process. Yet, it often takes at least a small injection of money
to get a programmer to bring open source software to the point of being
non-programmer friendly. So, that is kind of the state of things, because
most programmers are surviving in a commercial economy, and because most
components that make up OSS tend to be stitched together from many smaller
contributions, most of which where made to solve a specific problem, but
meet guidlines for inclusion in the greater system by the community.

User interface is getting better, though, in OSS. Examples like Ubuntu,
Socialtext, http://wagn.org/wagn/Wagn.org Drupal, are illustrative
