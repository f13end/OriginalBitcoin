Michel-

But the argument by the elite goes that without them, the wealth would not 
have been produced because they have some kind of integral and superior role 
to everyone else. So, the wealth is not extracted in that mindset, it was 
created. And people lucky enough to help with that should be glad to get 
some small part of what would not exist without a rich elite to make stuff 
happen. So, in this model, elites are not extractive. They are the main 
force of production.

And, at a place like Princeton, that seems obvious. All these busy people 
rushing around, writing papers, training students, all this growth, new 
buildings, and so on. And when I was there, did I ever think much that the 
mascot, the Tiger, went out and ate people and other creatures? No, it's 
more, how cute, a growing Tiger cub. Look all that growth and productivity 
as the tiger cub gets bigger. Wow, something special is happening here. Etc. 
Aren't we all so great to be part of that growth and wonder? We're going to 
make the world a better place by being tigers. It takes a long time to see 
past stuff like that if you are clueless like me. :-) (Nothing against 
tigers; and the cubs *are* cute, too.)

Of course, if people are poor and have no free time because all the wealth 
in society is concentrated, that's a bit of a self-fulfilling prophecy, 
isn't it?

As you point out, places that have had a more equal distribution of wealth 
have been more dynamic in a lot of ways. Even people who defend capitalism 
will admit, if pushed, that capitalism works better if wealth is broadly 
distributed, because that allows small businesses to form, which tend to be 
were most job growth and innovation come from.
