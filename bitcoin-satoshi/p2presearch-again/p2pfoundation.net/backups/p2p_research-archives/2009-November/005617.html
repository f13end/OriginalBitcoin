On Fri, Nov 6, 2009 at 11:58 PM, Ryan Lanham <rlanham1963 at gmail.com> wrote:

> Hi Michel,
>
> On the one hand you claim that power extracts wealth from those who
> rightfully earn it.  On the other, you claim from each according to his
> ability to each according to his need.  How are these different?  It would
> be a strange worldview that saw them not as not both focused on taking
> something from someone else by power.  That is not P2P.
>

the first principle is the one of a class society, and the extraction has
gotten various different forms; the second is the very definition of p2p, so
of course they are opposed ... but there are also, according to fiske's
system, which I use, the gift economy. Market pricing could be fair, the
exchange of equal value, in a system of equal producers, but you'll agree
that this is not the case today. The second one, where each freely gives
what he wants, and receives from a commons what he needs, is by definition
non-coercive, so I'm not sure where your question is, as you seem to say
that voluntary contributions and sharing are coercive? If it is coercively
taken from someone else, it is be defintion not peer to peer, but some form
of hierarchical allocation (even if it is done by the poor against the rich,
say)



>
> I have no problem with someone saying they are a communist and working for
> the state.  That is logical.  But to say one is for P2P and that one works
> with and for the state (or willingly takes its assets) is, to me, absurd.
> It negates the very idea.  One cannot possibly be a communist (or a
> capitalist) or neoliberal, if you prefer, though that term seems
> intentionally acrimonious while being largely non-descriptive (I know of no
> person who calls themselves a neoliberal though I know many economists and
> capitalists--like Robert Reich), while being formally for P2P. One cannot be
> for P2P in any strong sense and be conventional in political philosophical
> terms.
>


But that hybridity is unavoidable, certainly at present. So p2p works in
some circumstances, in the immaterial realm, but not yet in the physical one
(though we see the emergence of modalities of physical production that are
orienting themselves around design commons of course). But even more, my
conception of p2p was never anti-statist or anarchist. So, like the ego, I
accept the state as a given, and perhaps one day it can be outgrown. While
it exists, the commoners and civil society must ensure it is maximally at
their side, that's all. And do not confuse public funding with bureaucratic
selection by officials, there are many ways in which the funds of the public
could go back to the public in different governance and selection
modalities. So, to be clear, I disagree with you that the state's assets do
not belong to the people in general. Of course, at present, they are in the
hands of a special body of people and hugely favour elite interests, I
recognize that. But what seems inconceivable to me is just the opposite,
that as a p2p sympathizer, you would have no moral problems with the
funneling of public money to a elite, and would object to democratic funding
of the general interest? It is the very basis of social democracy that you
sometimes say you admire in scandinavia. P2P must look at the other
modalities in a ethical way, and have a preferential choice for those
modalities of the other systems that are most in harmony with it.

Arts and free culture could conceivably have at least 3 major modes of
funding: the public, the private i.e. the market, and itself; the latter
still very problematic today. So hybrid models seem best, and if we don't
want culture to be a pure commodity, public funding is good. Historically,
the arts have always benefitted from patronage and public funding, not just
from selling itself as a commodity.



>
> P2P does not allow for a politics of redistribution as I understand it.
> Why/how should it? Sharing is not redistribution...it is self driven, hence
> the threat of a tragedy of the commons.
>


of course, but it co-exists with other modalities, as argued above, so we
still have to choose. and the real argument is not against redistribution,
which has happened on a massive scale from the bottom to the top since the
80's (and as you say, that is uncontroversial, just look it up <g>, david
harvey's book on neoliberalism is a good place to start), but rather about
the direction of redistribution, which I prefer to be in the direction of
fairness and more equality.

>

> clearly is not.
>

not controversial to you, but hugely controversial to many people, including
many liberals that arer in no way com/socs. I'm not sure you are correct in
thinking that Kevin shares your preferences though. Kevin?

if you identify communism with the state, as you clearly seem to do, then of
course p2p has nothing to do it, though in a hybrid economy, it has to
compose with it; if you agree with fiske's definition of communal
sharehodling and the commons as p2p, then of course, you would reach
different conclusions

>
> What the problem here is that there is a mixture of politics (usually

I do not agree with Kevin's positions in all things, but I have never seen
him as a supporter of the rich, and of neoliberalism, but I think he can
best explain it himself. His mutualism is very close to socialism by the
way, it's individual based rather than collectivist, but it comes from the
same general thrust of politics.


>
> Spouting anti-market communist ideas on the one hand, and P2P on the other,
> does both a disservice in my view.  If a person is a communist, they should

> the other hand, if a person is for P2P, I cannot see how they can

> far as I can tell, wholly incompatible.

well again, if you define communism as statism, and I suspect for you it is
the same as stalinism, then of course you are right. I susptect there are
not many here who would define themselves as anti-market communists,
anyone???

and if some would, I'm pretty sure they would not agree, and be very much
opposed, to statist and totalitarian stalinism ..

>
> I am not trying to be argumentative.  I certainly have no issue with people
> being communists.  I do not personally agree with that political/economic
> philosophy, but I understand it.  It is coherent.  P2P, it seems to me, runs
> great risk at not being what it is because people are being sloppy with its
> moral rules.  Either it is a worldview/political philosophy or it isn't very
> interesting (to me). Others here have said it is a set of technologies.  I
> doubt that personally.  It seems to me it requires a position against
> coercion (which makes it inherently not communist) and it requires a moral
> commitment to sharing.  It is not inherently inconsistent with capitalism
> but it is socially so because it sets the growth of the commons above that
> of the individual.
>

peer to peer is the permissionless aggregation around the creation of common
value, whereby people can freely contribute, and share in the proceeds of
that common creation

it is both different from the capitalist market, and from stalinist state
socialism (or state capitalism, as other would say).

it has clearly a space today within the context of capitalist markets, more
so than it did in totalitarian statist societies, which usurped the commons
totally.
