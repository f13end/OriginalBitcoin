Hi Chris,

it may be missing in that text, but yes, it is one of the cornerstones of
the p2p-f vision as well,

one of my key arguments is that open knowledge/software/design communities
design differently, and think about machinery differently than corporations;
and this is even apart from the miniaturization that you describe, which
leads to the same effects,

global design commons, with more local and distirbuted manufacturing, based
on much lower-entry capital goods ... the global scaling of small group
dynamics, for both design and manufacturing, but in different ways, that
becomes competitive with centralized command and control models ... It is
because of what you rightly point out, that I speak of 'enterpreneurial
coalitions' rather than using another term

see http://p2pfoundation.net/Category:Design and
http://p2pfoundation.net/Category:Manufacturing for more detailed
presentations of material supporting my and your view,

Michel

> Interesting ideas around 'industrial revolution 2.0' (although it's
> probably more like 8.0...).
>
> One thing that I think you may be missing (based on my summary review of
> the blog post, ahem) is the distributed manufacturing component.   This
> has been a pet theory of mine for some time, partially because of some
> hobbies I have that require custom machining....
>
> Basically, the price of CNC and other automated machining tools has gone
> through the floor in the last 10 years.  It used to be that a CNC
> machining center would cost $150k and require a warehouse to run
> (including industrial electricity supplies).  Now you can get the same
> capability in a sub-$10k machine that fits and runs in your garage.   At
> some level, this is leading to a new kind of business, which is light
> industrial manufacturing, but highly distributed and on a very small
> scale.  On a personal level, I have been doing exactly this (as a
> hobbyist) for the better part of 8 years.  What's changed recently is
> that it is no longer necessary to acquire huge amounts of skill and
> knowledge before being productive.
>
> The extreme expression of this is what I have described as a 'Market of
> One', which is where highly automated CNC (and 3d printers) combine with
> easy to use design tools that allow for completely customized designs on
> a wide scale.   You order something only and it is 'printed out' at a
> corner shop or even in your house.
>
> There are several tentative examples of this already such as:
>
> http://www.emachineshop.com
>
> and
>
> http://www.shapeways.com/
>
> Of course, you may already be in tune with all this, but I'm just sayin'
>  ;-)
